HOST: The current political climate appears to be defined by conflict, wouldn't you say?

REPORTER: That's a fair assessment. Commentators describe the President's actions as a series of "relentless, utterly incoherent battles," putting the constitution under considerable pressure. This extends to his quest for retribution within the Justice Department. Even with a new Attorney General in place, the underlying demand for revenge seems to persist, with some believing his demands have become so extreme that even his most loyal appointees struggle to meet them. It indicates a deep-seated tension between political objectives and institutional norms.

REPORTER: Yes, Kevin Warsh’s nomination to the Federal Reserve is moving forward, despite a Senate committee member still planning to block it. The hearing for his nomination is scheduled for mid-April.

This latest strike underscores the ongoing human cost of the conflict and Russia's continued targeting of civilian infrastructure.

REPORTER: Precisely. Faced with a new energy shock driven by the war and rising gas and fuel prices, European nations are seriously reconsidering nuclear power. HOST: Moving to Africa, a story highlighting the challenges of urban development and social justice.

REPORTER: In Nigeria, people who were evicted to make way for the construction of the nation’s capital are accusing the government of failing to fulfill its promises to them. OpenAI states this deal aims to "create a space for a real, constructive conversation about the changes AI creates," suggesting a move towards shaping public narrative around AI.

HOST: This acquisition comes alongside some internal leadership changes at OpenAI, doesn't it?

REPORTER: It does. OpenAI President Fidji Simo is taking medical leave, leading to significant leadership shifts. Greg Brockman, a co-founder and current President, will now oversee product in Simo's absence.
